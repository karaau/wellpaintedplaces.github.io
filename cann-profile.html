Well Painted Places

In February of 2020, one month before the world would be engulfed by the coronavirus, Nathan Cann embarked on a journey to the Magdalen Islands. A French-speaking archipelago in the Gulf of Saint Lawrence, it sits isolated from the province of Quebec to which it belongs. Many of its inhabitants work seasonally in fishing, mining, and tourism—in the winter, they warm their bodies with ale.

Cann arrives at one of the most inhospitable times of the year, some of the islands being inaccessible due to harsh snowstorms. Residing on Cap-aux-Meules, the most populated island in the archipelago, he peers outside the window of his room and sees nothing but the blinding whiteness of snow moving at light speed. He notes that upon his arrival he is greeted by well-painted places—well-kempt houses richly adorned in radiant colours. The frigid winds pelt snow and ice against the siding, but the colours act like warding amulets to protect the people inside and enliven those outside.
